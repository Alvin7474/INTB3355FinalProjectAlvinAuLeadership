Section I

Introduction to Leadership

Leadership has long been considered one of the most researched yet least understood aspects of organizational life. As a result, we commonly view leaders as individuals gifted with something unique such as charisma, confidence, or the ability to inspire. However, this course provided me the opportunity to challenge that belief from Day One.

During the Fall Semester, I participated in INTB 3355 which presented to me a more detailed and factually based understanding of leadership one founded upon the individual's deliberate actions and their willingness to recognize the need for a flexible and culturally aware style. In essence, leadership is no longer viewed as an aspect of who you are but rather as to what you do, how you go about doing it, and your ability to assess the situation and make appropriate adjustments.

Three articles published in the Harvard Business Review anchored much of my reflection. The first, by Peterson, Abramson, and Stutman (2020), explores the science behind leadership style introducing the concept of power and attractiveness markers and making the case that a truly blended style creates lasting presence. The second, by Wezowski (2018), examines how microexpressions and body language serve as a universal language, even when cultural norms vary dramatically across borders. The third, by Leinwand, Mani, and Sheppard (2022), zooms out from the individual to the collective arguing that the future of leadership lies not in any single executive's capabilities, but in how leadership teams are built, focused, and made to function as a unified whole.

Together, these readings reshaped how I think about influence, communication, and my own development as a future business professional in an increasingly global world.

Section II

Reflection on Initial Soft Skills & Leadership Abilities

As a result of participating in this course prior to beginning class, I possessed a somewhat confident albeit limited image of my own leadership attributes. I had experience assuming various group-related tasks; I felt comfortable speaking during team sessions and presenting ideas to groups of peers. Additionally, I had a high level of work ethic and a goal-oriented focus which frequently contributed to the successful completion of projects.

However, if I were to speak honestly regarding my strengths my strengths were very focused in the task arena (the technical and organizational aspects) and I had far fewer strengths related to the relational arena. I could accomplish tasks with ease, however, I did not consistently understand how my approach impacted those around me. Most often, I used direct statements in presentations; I would move rapidly over other perspectives within meeting environments; and I would infrequently modify my communication style based upon whom I was communicating with.

Using the framework provided by Peterson et al. (2020), I believe I would place myself solidly in the Lean Powerful category on the leadership style continuum relying heavily on power markers such as assertive statements, directness, and commanding pace, without enough counterbalance from attractiveness markers like empathy, inclusive language, and collaborative questions.

💪

Initial Strengths

Strong task orientation, confidence in presenting ideas, ability to drive group projects, clear verbal communication, and comfort with structure and deadlines.

🔍

Areas for Improvement

Limited flexibility in communication style, insufficient attention to nonverbal cues, tendency to favor statements over questions, and low cross-cultural reading ability.

🌐

Cultural Awareness

While I had some exposure to diverse peers, I lacked a systematic framework for reading and adapting to cultural differences in professional communication contexts.

"A great leadership style can make people appear more competent than they truly are, and a poor style can drag down a superior skill set."

Peterson, Abramson & Stutman, HBR (2020)

This quote hit home for me. I had assumed technical competence and a confident demeanor were enough. The research suggested otherwise and I was ready to test that assumption over the course of the semester.

Section III

Description of Learning Throughout the Course

One of the greatest paradigm shifts that occurred during my participation in this course was recognizing that leadership style is not simply a function of personality. A person's personality is typically consistent throughout their life it is who you are at your core. On the other hand, style is behavioral it is what you do, how often you perform that action, and when you perform that action. Recognizing this distinction has created an entire realm of opportunities for me to pursue intentional development.

The Power–Attractiveness Spectrum

Peterson et al. (2020) introduced me to the concept of social markers the behavioral signals we send that communicate our status to others. These fall into two categories: power markers (associated with confidence, competence, and charisma, but also arrogance and intimidation) and attractiveness markers (associated with approachability and likability, but potentially with submissiveness if overused).

⚡

Power Markers

Interrupting to make a point, using declarative statements, setting an agenda, taking the head of the table, using intense and confident language, commanding pacing.

🤝

Attractiveness Markers

Asking questions over making statements, using "we" and "our," holding doors, restating what others say, maintaining open body language, and expressing empathy.

⚖️

The Blended Style

The most effective leaders dynamically integrate both sets of markers projecting confidence while remaining approachable. This is the hallmark of genuine "presence."

📌

Key Insight from Peterson et al. (2020)

True leadership presence is not about dominance OR warmth it requires both simultaneously and dynamically. The same leader may need to project authority in one meeting and collaborative openness in the next. The ability to shift fluidly between these registers defines the elite performer.

Cross-Cultural Intelligence & Microexpressions

Wezowski (2018) opened a complementary dimension of leadership learning: how to read people accurately across cultural boundaries. In international business, leaders cannot assume that the body language norms of their home culture apply everywhere. What signals confidence in the United States may signal disrespect in Japan, or warmth in Brazil may come across as inappropriate familiarity in Qatar.

The key insight from Wezowski is that while body language is culturally variable, microexpressions are universal. These brief, involuntary flashes of facial expression lasting only fractions of a second reveal genuine emotion regardless of cultural background. A skilled leader learns to read these microexpressions to calibrate responses, detect misalignment, and build real trust across cultural divides.

🌍

Key Insight from Wezowski (2018)

Body language is cultural, but emotions are universal. Microexpressions bypass cultural masking, offering leaders a direct window into how others truly feel. Developing this skill is a competitive advantage in global business contexts especially in cultures where overt emotional expression is suppressed by social norms.

Cultural Variations in Leadership Style

One of the most eye-opening sections of Peterson et al. (2020) was "The Culture Effect" a discussion of how the same style marker carries different meanings in different cultural contexts. Eye contact, attire, note-taking behavior, and seating arrangements all function as status signals but what they signal differs dramatically by geography and context.

Eye Contact

Culture-Dependent Status Signaling

In the U.S., direct eye contact signals confidence. In Japan, it can be read as insubordinate. In Brazil, it signals appropriate deference. The behavior is the same; the meaning is not.

Attire

Power Through Dress

A tech founder wearing a t-shirt to meet investment bankers in the U.S. projects a power marker. In many African cultures, traditional tribal dress is the power marker. Context transforms meaning.

Note-Taking

Respect vs. Deference

In the U.S., taking notes in senior meetings can seem overly deferential. In South Korea, not taking notes may be seen as disrespectful as if you don't value what's being said.

Reinventing Leadership: From Individual to Collective

While Peterson et al. and Wezowski focused on the individual leader, Leinwand, Mani, and Sheppard (2022) introduced me to a higher-level challenge: how do organizations collectively lead through transformation? Drawing on research at 12 prominent global firms including Microsoft, Hitachi, and Philips they identified six paradoxical skills that tomorrow's leaders must balance simultaneously:

🔭

Strategic Executor

No longer can a leader be either a visionary or an operator. Today's executives must be both imagining the future and rolling up their sleeves to implement it.

💻

Tech-Savvy Humanist

Leaders must harness technology while never losing sight of the people it serves balancing digital fluency with deep empathy and human-centered thinking.

🌍

Globally Minded Localist

Effective leaders understand global forces but translate strategy through the lens of local culture, regulation, and community as Philips's Carla Kriwet described her "United Nations" team.

🦸

Humble Hero

Confidence and humility are not opposites in great leadership they are partners. The best leaders lead boldly while remaining open to being challenged and proved wrong.

🏛️

Key Insight from Leinwand, Mani & Sheppard (2022)

Leadership transformation is not a solo act. CEOs must build teams that cover the full range of paradoxical leadership capabilities collectively no single person needs to master every tension, but the team as a whole must. This shifts the focus from individual development to collective architecture: who is in the room, how they are focused, and whether they trust each other enough to lead boldly together.

Particularly striking was the article's observation about team focus: the best leadership teams deliberately protect time for transformation, ensuring that urgent daily operations do not crowd out the important long-term priorities. As Philips's CEO Frans van Houten put it, the imperative is to "both perform and transform" and holding both simultaneously is itself a leadership skill.

Section IV

Application of Learning in Practical Contexts

Applying these concepts to actual practice required conscious effort and exposed just how entrenched many of my default behaviors were. Below are the primary situations in which I utilized the course material.

🗣️

Group Project Communication

I began consciously incorporating partnership language replacing "my idea" with "our direction" and "I think" with "what if we considered." Group members noted a shift toward more collaborative dynamics.

👀

Cross-Cultural Reading

While working with peers from different cultural backgrounds, I practiced scanning for microexpressions when verbal communication felt unclear particularly subtle discomfort signals that I had previously missed.

🎤

Presentations & Meetings

I experimented with "reading the room" before choosing my style approach leaning powerful when establishing credibility with skeptical audiences, and leaning attractive when facilitating consensus-building sessions.

🪞

Self-Monitoring

I began reviewing recorded presentations for my marker usage tracking how often I made declarative statements vs. asked questions, and noting instances where my nonverbal behavior contradicted my intended message.

👥

Team Leadership Thinking

Inspired by Leinwand et al. (2022), I began approaching group projects not as collections of individual contributors but as small leadership teams deliberately thinking about who covered which paradoxical capability and how to keep the group focused on the big goal, not just the task list.

"She was known for her ability to 'disagree agreeably' which helped her create unlikely friendships with more-conservative judges and foster loyal followership."

Peterson et al. (2020), on Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg's blended leadership style

"The only way to lead the company was to do the work I felt was required for the organization to move forward not spend all day responding to what others provided."

CEO quoted in Leinwand, Mani & Sheppard, HBR (2022)

This quote from Leinwand et al. challenged me to think about my own tendencies in group settings how often was I reacting to what others put in front of me, rather than proactively shaping the direction? Bringing a more transformation-oriented mindset to even small-scale team projects made me a noticeably more engaged and effective contributor.

The Ginsburg example resonated with me deeply. The ability to hold a strong conviction while remaining genuinely open and respectful is a rare combination and one I began actively practicing in discussions where I disagreed with peers or had to advocate for a minority position.

Similarly, Wezowski's guidance on microexpression reading changed how I approach one-on-one conversations. Rather than focusing solely on what someone is saying, I started attending more closely to what their face is communicating in the half-second before they speak. This small behavioral shift has already improved my ability to sense misalignment early and adjust my approach accordingly.

Section VI

Conclusion

Upon entering INTB 3355, I perceived leadership as largely an issue of knowledge and results if you knew the subject matter and produced tangible results, then the remainder would naturally fall into place. While this course challenged that perception and challenged it significantly it did so in a respectful manner.

Leadership is, at its core, a communicative act. It happens in the space between people in the words chosen, the pauses kept, the eye contact made or avoided, and the split-second expressions that pass across a face before a word is spoken. It is shaped by culture, filtered through bias, and magnified or diminished by the style of the person doing it.

Through the lens of Peterson, Abramson, and Stutman's research on power and attractiveness markers, I gained a precise vocabulary for understanding and improving my own leadership presence. Through Wezowski's work on microexpressions and cross-cultural body language, I developed a more sensitive and universal tool for reading the humans I am privileged to work alongside. And through Leinwand, Mani, and Sheppard's research on reinventing leadership teams, I came to understand that the highest form of leadership is not individual excellence it is collective architecture: assembling diverse teams, focusing them on transformation, and building the trust that makes bold action possible.

The most important lesson I carry forward is this: style is not fixed, and neither is leadership. Like Vince Lombardi learning to walk the halls at night and sit with his players, like Ruth Bader Ginsburg mastering the art of disagreeing agreeably the greatest leaders are those who remain students of the human experience and are always willing to adjust, grow, and lead differently when the situation demands it.

I leave this course not as a finished leader, but as a more intentional one.

"Dynamically integrating a broader range of powerful and attractive markers in everyday interactions can make a big difference in how we are perceived."
